The Ethics of Human Gene Editing

Question 1 of 15
Comprehension
What is CRISPR technology used for?
Explanation

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a revolutionary gene-editing technology that acts like "molecular scissors," allowing scientists to cut and modify specific DNA sequences with unprecedented precision and efficiency.

Question 2 of 15
Vocabulary
What does "germline editing" mean?
Explanation

Germline editing involves modifying genes in sperm, eggs, or embryos, which means the changes can be passed on to offspring. This is ethically controversial because it affects not just the individual but all future descendants.

Question 5 of 15
Comprehension
What is the main ethical concern about "designer babies"?
Explanation

Designer babies raise profound ethical questions: Who decides what traits are "desirable"? Will gene editing be accessible only to the wealthy, creating genetic inequality? What about unintended consequences? These concerns reflect deeper questions about human nature and equality.

Question 6 of 15
Vocabulary
What does "eugenics" mean?
Explanation

Eugenics has a dark history, associated with forced sterilization and genocide. Modern gene editing raises concerns about "neo-eugenics"—the risk that genetic selection could be used to discriminate against certain groups or reinforce harmful biases about what constitutes "normal" or "superior."

Question 7 of 15
Grammar: Future Forms
Which sentence correctly uses future forms?
Explanation

Both are grammatically correct. "Will have become" (Future Perfect) emphasizes completion by a specific time, while "will become" (Simple Future) is more general. The choice depends on whether you want to emphasize the completion or just the future state.

Question 8 of 15
Critical Thinking
Is there a difference between therapeutic and enhancement gene editing?
Explanation

This distinction is crucial. Therapeutic editing (curing disease) has broader ethical support, while enhancement editing (creating superior traits) raises concerns about inequality, coercion, and redefining human nature. The line between them can be blurry, making ethical frameworks complex.

Question 9 of 15
Vocabulary
What does "off-target effects" mean in gene editing?
Explanation

Off-target effects are a major safety concern in gene editing. Even precise tools like CRISPR can accidentally edit unintended DNA sequences, potentially causing new mutations or health problems. This is why extensive testing is ethically necessary before human applications.

The Future Continuous ("will be grappling") emphasizes an ongoing, continuous process. This is ideal for discussing how society will continue to face and work through ethical challenges as technology advances.

Question 11 of 15
Comprehension
What was the controversy surrounding He Jiankui's gene-edited babies?
Explanation

In 2018, Chinese scientist He Jiankui announced he had created the first gene-edited human babies, modifying the CCR5 gene to confer HIV resistance. This violated ethical guidelines, lacked proper oversight, and sparked global outrage, leading to his imprisonment and highlighting the urgent need for international gene-editing regulations.

Question 12 of 15
Vocabulary
What does "informed consent" mean in medical ethics?
Explanation

Informed consent is a cornerstone of medical ethics. For gene editing, this is particularly complex: Can embryos consent? Can parents consent on behalf of future generations who will carry edited genes? These questions highlight the ethical challenges of germline editing.

Question 13 of 15
Grammar: Future Forms
Which sentence is grammatically correct?
Explanation

Both are grammatically correct. "Will have developed" (Future Perfect) emphasizes completion by 2040, while "will develop" (Simple Future) is more general. Both are acceptable; the choice depends on your emphasis.

Question 14 of 15
Critical Thinking
Should there be a global moratorium on germline gene editing in humans?
Explanation

A moratorium is a middle-ground approach: it pauses germline editing while allowing somatic cell research and therapy development, giving time for safety improvements, ethical consensus, and international regulation. Many scientific organizations support this cautious approach.
